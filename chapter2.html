Chapter 2: Bonding and Structure of Chemical Compounds

In this chapter, we will delve into the various types of chemical bonds and the structures that result from these bonds. Understanding bonding is essential for grasping the properties and behaviors of compounds.

Ionic Bonding

Ionic bonds form when electrons are transferred from one atom to another, resulting in the formation of ions. These oppositely charged ions attract each other, creating a strong bond.

  • Characteristics: High melting and boiling points, conduct electricity when dissolved in water.
  • Examples: Sodium chloride (NaCl), magnesium oxide (MgO).

Covalent Bonding

Covalent bonds form when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons. These bonds can create molecules with diverse shapes and properties.

  • Characteristics: Lower melting and boiling points compared to ionic compounds, do not conduct electricity.
  • Examples: Water (H2O), carbon dioxide (CO2).

Metallic Bonding

Metallic bonds occur between metal atoms. In these bonds, electrons are delocalized, meaning they can move freely between atoms. This 'sea of electrons' provides metals with their characteristic properties.

  • Characteristics: High electrical and thermal conductivity, malleability, ductility.
  • Examples: Iron (Fe), aluminum (Al).

Intermolecular Forces

Beyond ionic, covalent, and metallic bonds, intermolecular forces also play a critical role in the properties of compounds. These forces include hydrogen bonds, dipole-dipole interactions, and London dispersion forces.

  • Hydrogen Bonds: Strong type of dipole-dipole interaction, occurs in molecules containing H bonded to N, O, or F.
  • Dipole-Dipole Interactions: Attractions between oppositely charged regions of polar molecules.
  • London Dispersion Forces: Weakest intermolecular force, present in all molecules, significant in nonpolar compounds.
