Chapter 3: Nomenclature of Chemical Compounds

In this chapter, we will explore the rules and conventions used to name chemical compounds. Proper nomenclature is essential for clear communication in chemistry.

Ionic Compounds

Ionic compounds are named based on the ions involved. The cation (positive ion) is named first, followed by the anion (negative ion).

  • Simple Ions: The name of the metal ion remains the same as the element (e.g., Na+ is sodium). Non-metal ions end in -ide (e.g., Cl- is chloride).
  • Transition Metals: For metals that can form more than one ion, the charge is specified with Roman numerals (e.g., Fe2+ is iron(II), Fe3+ is iron(III)).
  • Polyatomic Ions: These ions have specific names, like sulfate (SO42-) and nitrate (NO3-).

Covalent Compounds

Covalent compounds, also known as molecular compounds, are named using prefixes to indicate the number of each type of atom present.

  • Prefixes: mono- (1), di- (2), tri- (3), tetra- (4), penta- (5), hexa- (6), hepta- (7), octa- (8), nona- (9), deca- (10).
  • Example: CO2 is carbon dioxide, and P2O5 is diphosphorus pentoxide.

Acids

Acids have specific naming conventions based on their anions.

  • Binary Acids: Composed of hydrogen and one other element. Named with the prefix "hydro-" and the suffix "-ic" (e.g., HCl is hydrochloric acid).
  • Oxyacids: Contain hydrogen, oxygen, and another element. Named based on the polyatomic ion: "-ate" ions become "-ic" acids, and "-ite" ions become "-ous" acids (e.g., H2SO4 is sulfuric acid, H2SO3 is sulfurous acid).

Organic Compounds

Organic compounds are named according to the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) rules. This involves identifying the longest carbon chain and naming based on the functional groups present.

  • Alkanes: Simplest hydrocarbons, named with the suffix "-ane" (e.g., methane, ethane).
  • Alkenes and Alkynes: Contain double or triple bonds, named with the suffix "-ene" or "-yne" (e.g., ethene, ethyne).
  • Functional Groups: Specific groups of atoms within molecules that determine the chemical properties of the molecule (e.g., alcohols end in "-ol", like ethanol).
