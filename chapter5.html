Chapter 5: Acids and Bases

In this chapter, we will explore the properties, theories, and reactions of acids and bases. Understanding these concepts is essential in both theoretical and applied chemistry.

Properties of Acids and Bases

Acids and bases have distinct properties that can be observed and measured:

  • Acids: Taste sour, turn blue litmus paper red, and react with metals to produce hydrogen gas.
  • Bases: Taste bitter, feel slippery, turn red litmus paper blue, and react with acids to form water and salts.

Theories of Acids and Bases

Several theories have been proposed to explain the behavior of acids and bases:

  • Arrhenius Theory: Acids produce hydrogen ions (H+) in aqueous solution, while bases produce hydroxide ions (OH-).
  • Brønsted-Lowry Theory: Acids are proton donors, and bases are proton acceptors.
  • Lewis Theory: Acids are electron pair acceptors, and bases are electron pair donors.

pH Scale

The pH scale measures the acidity or basicity of a solution. It ranges from 0 to 14, with 7 being neutral:

  • pH < 7: Acidic solutions (higher concentration of H+ ions).
  • pH = 7: Neutral solutions (equal concentration of H+ and OH- ions).
  • pH > 7: Basic solutions (higher concentration of OH- ions).

Acid-Base Reactions

Acid-base reactions involve the transfer of protons between reactants:

  • Neutralization: Acid reacts with a base to produce a salt and water (e.g., HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H2O).
  • Titration: Technique used to determine the concentration of an acid or base by reacting it with a solution of known concentration.

Buffer Solutions

Buffers are solutions that resist changes in pH when small amounts of acid or base are added. They are crucial in maintaining the pH of biological and chemical systems:

  • Components: A weak acid and its conjugate base, or a weak base and its conjugate acid.
  • Example: Acetic acid (CH3COOH) and sodium acetate (CH3COONa) form a buffer solution.

Applications of Acids and Bases

Acids and bases have numerous applications in various fields:

  • Industry: Acids like sulfuric acid are used in manufacturing, and bases like sodium hydroxide are used in soap production.
  • Biology: Enzyme activity is pH-dependent, and the human body uses buffers to maintain blood pH.
  • Environmental Science: Understanding acid rain and its effects on ecosystems.
